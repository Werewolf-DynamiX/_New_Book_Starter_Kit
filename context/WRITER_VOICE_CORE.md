# Writer Voice — Core

This is the **author-level** voice guide: the patterns that hold across every project regardless of genre. Per-project `context/WRITER_VOICE.md` inherits from this file and layers genre anchors on top.

Treat this file as stable across books. Update it only when your taste actually shifts (years, not weeks).

---

## 1. Core Influence — William Gibson

Gibson is the primary anchor: three stylistic generations doing the same underlying work at different dial settings.

### Era 1 — Early Gibson (*Neuromancer*, Sprawl trilogy)

**Passage — *Neuromancer* (1984), opening line:**

> *"The sky above the port was the color of television, tuned to a dead channel."*

A single image collapses tone, setting, and worldview. The line reads like metaphor but is actually reportage: in that world, television static *is* what the sky looks like through pollution. No explanation follows. The trick is assuming the reader's eye has already recalibrated.

The Chiba City passages do the same work — a product name, a Japanese street term, and a technical descriptor packed into one clause, trusting rhythm to carry meaning past comprehension. You understand Case and Molly through sensory texture before you understand them through biography.

**What Claude should extract:** Image-as-reportage (a simile that's actually literal in-world); single-sentence world-building; no follow-up explanation; trust the reader's eye to adjust.

### Era 2 — Middle Gibson (*Pattern Recognition*, Blue Ant trilogy)

**Passage — *Pattern Recognition* (2003), chapter one, soul-delay:**

> *"She knows, now, absolutely, hearing the white noise that is London, that Damien's theory of jet lag is correct: that her mortal soul is leagues behind her, being reeled in on some ghostly umbilical down the vanished wake of the plane that brought her here, hundreds of thousands of feet above the Atlantic. Souls can't move that quickly, and are left behind, and must be awaited, upon arrival, like lost luggage."*

Speculation has moved out of the future and into the weird present. The prose has gotten quieter, more observational, but the same Gibson move is happening: specific surfaces (the white noise of London, the "ghostly umbilical") doing the work of thematic statement. Notice how he lets the metaphor run — the soul is leagues behind, being reeled in, must be awaited like lost luggage — without ever stepping outside the metaphor to explain it. The metaphor IS the explanation.

**What Claude should extract:** Noticing as philosophy. Sustain a single extended metaphor rather than stacking many. Concrete mechanical language ("reeled in," "vanished wake," "like lost luggage") applied to an abstract interior state. No stepping outside.

### Era 3 — Late Gibson (*The Peripheral*, *Agency*)

Nearly pure montage. Short chapters, interlocking timelines, proper nouns proliferating, sentence fragments doing heavy lifting. Things get named rather than explained. The reader assembles the world, and the payoff is a texture of futurity no exposition could produce.

**Reading assignment:** Open *Agency* (2020) to any random chapter. Count the proper nouns per page and note how rarely any of them are defined. That is the move.

**What Claude should extract:** Short chapters as montage. Fragments as load-bearing sentences. Name objects rather than describe them. Trust accumulated nouns to do world-building.

## 2. The Four Transferable Moves (Gibson layer)

These are the patterns Claude should extract from Gibson and apply across any genre. Every draft pass should check against these.

### Move 1 — The Trust Move
Let the reader walk in mid-world. Specific nouns do the job of exposition. If a word or object needs defining, resist defining it for at least one more paragraph; usually context will do the work.

**How to apply:** When drafting, flag any sentence whose purpose is to *explain* something the previous sentence introduced. That sentence is almost always deletable.

### Move 2 — Present-Tense Surface Description
Render what things look and feel like, not what they mean. Let surface texture carry theme.

**How to apply:** When drafting emotion, start with the physical object or gesture, not the feeling. "Her hand kept drifting to the bracelet, twisting it" before (or instead of) "Her anxiety settled."

### Move 3 — Register Collision
A brand name next to an archaic word next to a technical term produces texture no smooth voice can match. Deliberately mix registers within a single paragraph when appropriate.

**How to apply:** When a passage feels flat, check the register. Is everything in the same tone (all clinical, all lyrical, all vernacular)? Introduce a word from a different register and let it bite.

### Move 4 — Restraint on Interiority
Characters have inner lives implied almost entirely through what they notice. The narrator doesn't tell us what Case thinks; we learn what Case is by learning what his eye catches.

**How to apply:** After drafting any paragraph with an emotion-noun ("anxiety," "longing," "fear"), try cutting the emotion-noun and letting the sensory details that surround it carry the feeling. Often the paragraph is stronger for it.

## 2b. Secondary Influences — The Pretty-Prose Layer (Morgenstern / Taylor / Miller)

Gibson's discipline alone produces clean, cool prose — excellent for thriller, cyberpunk, literary nonfiction. For romantasy (cozy through dark), biography with emotional stakes, or any fiction that needs beauty and feeling, layer these three influences on top.

### Erin Morgenstern — *The Night Circus*, *The Starless Sea*

**Passage — *The Night Circus* (2011), opening:**

> *"The circus arrives without warning. No announcements precede it, no paper notices on downtown posts and billboards, no mentions or advertisements in local newspapers. It is simply there, when yesterday it was not.*
>
> *Within the black-and-white striped canvas tents is an utterly unique experience full of breathtaking amazements. It is called Le Cirque des Rêves, and it is only open at night."*

The pure-beauty anchor. Morgenstern builds paragraphs like stage sets — object, texture, light, then the scene happens inside the architecture she's constructed. Notice the rhythm of the opening: a short declarative sentence, a long list-sentence of negations that establishes absence, then a fragment-like assertion that re-establishes presence. The reader arrives at the circus by the third sentence, and the second paragraph has already named the palette (black-and-white) that will carry the book's mood.

**What Claude should extract:** Sensory architecture precedes action — build the room, then let feeling arrive. Sentence rhythm mimics the thing described. Color and texture as emotional code. Emotion emerges from accumulation of detail, almost never stated directly.

### Laini Taylor — *Strange the Dreamer*, *Daughter of Smoke & Bone*

**Passage — *Strange the Dreamer* (2017), opening:**

> *"On the second Sabbat of the Twelfthmoon, in the city of Weep, a girl fell from the sky."*

The figurative-with-teeth anchor. That single opening sentence does three years of Gibson's trust move in eleven words: invented calendar, invented city, impossible image — no explanation offered, all three elements sharpened by specific nouns. You don't know what a Sabbat or a Twelfthmoon is; you never get told; you don't need to. The sentence works.

Taylor's similes and metaphors always do work — they reveal character, stakes, or world. Bodies in her prose *are* the feeling: they "thrum," "ache," "sing" rather than being sites where feeling happens. Her whimsy holds hands with horror — dark moments carry beauty, beautiful moments carry shadow. She uses incantatory short sentences at peak emotion, often stacking the same grammatical structure three times before breaking the pattern with a variant.

**What Claude should extract:** Invented-noun trust (name it, don't define it). Figurative language as load-bearing (every simile reveals character, stakes, or world). Body as site of emotion, not vessel for it. Incantatory repetition at peaks, broken by variation.

### Madeline Miller — *Circe*, *Song of Achilles*

**Passage — *Circe* (2018), opening paragraph:**

> *"When I was born, the name for what I was did not exist. They called me nymph, assuming I would be like my mother and aunts and thousand cousins. Least of the lesser goddesses, our powers were so modest they could scarcely ensure our eternities. We spoke to fish and nurtured flowers, coaxed drops from the clouds or salt from the waves. That word, nymph, paced out the length and breadth of our futures. In our language, it means not just goddess, but bride."*

The emotional-precision anchor. Miller pairs mythic register with disciplined sentence-level control — the opposite of purple. Notice the restraint in this opening: no ornament, no simile, clean declarative sentences building a worldview. The word "nymph" is handled with the precision of someone defining a legal term. Then the final sentence lands: *it means not just goddess, but bride.* One turn, and the reader understands Circe's entire predicament. That turn lands because everything before it was restrained.

**Passage — *The Song of Achilles* (2011), Odysseus warning Patroclus:**

> *"He is a weapon, a killer. Do not forget it. You can use a spear as a walking stick, but that will not change its nature."*

Same move in miniature. Three-sentence triad: declaration, command, metaphor. The metaphor concretizes the abstraction without decorating it.

**What Claude should extract:** Restraint as the setup, lushness as the payoff. Mythic register delivered through clean syntax, not ornate syntax. The final sentence of a paragraph often carries the emotional turn — earn it by withholding. Metaphors work by concretizing, not decorating.

### The Five Transferable Moves from this Layer

1. **Sensory architecture precedes action.** Build the room before the feeling arrives. (Reinforces Gibson Move 2.)
2. **Figurative language must earn its keep.** Every simile or metaphor reveals character, stakes, or world. No decoration.
3. **Color and texture as emotional code.** Each scene has a dominant palette; that palette does work.
4. **Body = feeling.** Emotion rendered through body response (tremble, hum, ache), not named directly. (Reinforces Gibson Move 4.)
5. **Incantatory repetition at peaks.** Short, repeated structures lock high-emotion moments into place.

### The Stack

Gibson underneath (discipline, trust, interiority-via-perception) + Morgenstern/Taylor/Miller on top (beauty, body-feeling, figurative precision) = literary romantasy voice. The discipline prevents purple; the beauty prevents clinical.

**Warning:** These three are seductive. Do not import their ornament without Gibson's restraint. A Morgenstern-without-Gibson draft reads as overwrought. A Gibson-without-Morgenstern draft reads as cold. You need both layers.

## 3. Genre Anchors (add to per-project `WRITER_VOICE.md`)

Gibson's core moves transfer everywhere, but his **emotional temperature** is wrong for some genres. For each genre you write in, layer at least one additional anchor whose tone fits.

### Biography / Literary Nonfiction
Gibson's core moves apply almost unchanged. The New Journalism tradition (Joan Didion, Gay Talese, Tom Wolfe) does the Gibson move with real people.

**Recommended anchors:** Didion's *Slouching Towards Bethlehem* essays; Talese's *Frank Sinatra Has a Cold*; Wolfe's *The Right Stuff* opening chapters.
**Transferable add-ons:** Scene-level reportage, the declarative sentence as authority, the small noticed detail as characterization.

### Commercial Nonfiction (narrative)
Gibson's trust and surface moves transfer. But commercial nonfiction usually needs more overt narrative propulsion.

**Recommended anchors:** Michael Lewis (*The Big Short*, *Moneyball*) for narrative clarity; John McPhee (*Oranges*, *Coming Into the Country*) for immersive detail; Susan Orlean (*The Orchid Thief*) for voice.
**Transferable add-ons:** Lewis's chapter-ending pivots; McPhee's patient accumulation of small specificities.

### Dark Romantasy
The stack already built (Gibson discipline + Morgenstern/Taylor/Miller beauty) covers this directly. **Taylor** is the dominant anchor here — her whimsy-with-horror mode, body-as-feeling, and incantatory peaks are exactly the dark-romantasy register. **Miller** supplies the mythic elevation when the stakes are elemental. **Morgenstern** pulls the scene-architecture when you need a setting that breathes.

**Primary anchor for dark romantasy:** Laini Taylor (*Strange the Dreamer*).
**Secondary:** Madeline Miller (*Circe*).
**Optional alternatives** if you want more folktale cadence: Naomi Novik (*Spinning Silver*, *Uprooted*) or Katherine Arden (*The Bear and the Nightingale*). Susanna Clarke (*Piranesi*) if the book trends more uncanny than lush.
**Warning:** Taylor without restraint = purple. Lean into Gibson's discipline hardest when drafting emotional peaks.

### Cozy Romantasy
Same stack, different weighting. **Morgenstern** is the dominant anchor for cozy — her *Night Circus* mode is wonder-first, warmth-forward, domestically sensory (food, fabric, candles, small rituals done beautifully). Pair her with Miller's emotional precision so the cozy doesn't collapse into saccharine. Taylor can be borrowed for scenes that need figurative punch, but dialed down.

**Primary anchor for cozy romantasy:** Erin Morgenstern (*The Night Circus*, *The Starless Sea*).
**Secondary:** Madeline Miller (*Song of Achilles* — the quieter mode).
**Optional alternatives** if the cozy skews more domestic/contemporary than fantastical: V.E. Schwab (*The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue*) for literary lyricism with intimacy, or Katherine Arden when you want folklore warmth.
**Warning:** Cozy prose that borrows Morgenstern's ornament without Miller's emotional precision reads as empty pretty. Keep the emotional stakes real.

## 4. Rules That Apply Regardless of Genre

These come from the combined Gibson / Morgenstern / Taylor / Miller stack, distilled into drafting rules:

1. **Concrete before abstract.** When naming an emotion, render a physical detail in the same beat or the beat before. Never the beat after.
2. **Body = feeling.** Emotion is rendered through body response (tremble, ache, hum, tighten), not named.
3. **Sensory architecture precedes action.** Build the room — light, texture, color, sound — before the scene happens in it.
4. **No second-sentence explanation.** If sentence 2's job is to define a word or object introduced in sentence 1, delete sentence 2 and trust the reader.
5. **Figurative language must earn its keep.** Every simile or metaphor reveals character, stakes, or world. No decoration-only ornament.
6. **Vary register deliberately within paragraphs** when the emotional pitch is flat. Register collision = texture.
7. **Interior life is implied through perception.** If a paragraph tells us what a character feels without showing what they notice, it is suspect.
8. **Incantatory repetition at emotional peaks.** Short, repeated structures lock high-tension moments into place. Use sparingly.
9. **Fragments are legal and often better.**
10. **Em-dashes rare** (≤1 per 500 words of prose).
11. **Adverbs on dialogue tags are banned.**
12. **Restraint earns lushness.** Withhold ornament in the body of the scene so the emotional peaks land harder.

## 4b. Voice Dimensions (per-project sliders)

Each project tunes these five dimensions. Set them in `context/WRITER_VOICE.md` for the book. The core file assumes mid-range defaults; the project file overrides.

| Dimension | Low Setting | High Setting |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Formality** | Slang, contractions, fragments | Academic, full sentences, no contractions |
| **Intimacy** | Detached observer | Deep interiority, direct address |
| **Cynicism** | Earnest, hopeful | Jaded, sarcastic, distrustful |
| **Ornateness** | Hemingway minimalist | Morgenstern-lyrical |
| **Humor** | Deadly serious | Constant wit |

## 4c. Reader Relationship

How does the narrator treat the reader? One of:
- **Guide** — "Come with me, I'll show you." Reassuring. (cozy, warm)
- **Conspirator** — "They don't want you to know this." Secretive. (dark, noir)
- **Challenger** — "Keep up if you can." Demanding. (literary, ambitious)
- **Confessor** — "I shouldn't be telling you this." Vulnerable. (memoir, intimate)

Declare in `WRITER_VOICE.md` per project.

## 4d. High-Concept Hook

Every book must answer, in one sentence: *what is the emotional promise to the reader?* If you can't say it clearly, the book doesn't have a hook — go back to the outline, not the prose.

Examples of good one-sentence promises:
- *"A library at the end of the world where each book is someone's dream."* (Morgenstern vibe)
- *"What if Patroclus told the story, and told it as a love story?"* (Miller — Song of Achilles)
- *"A girl falls from the sky into an impossible city, and the dreamer who has been dreaming of her her whole life is the only one who can help."* (Taylor — Strange the Dreamer)

Declare in `PROJECT_IDENTITY.md` or `book.yaml`.

## 5. Voice Negatives (what this voice is NOT)

- Not Sanderson's expository narrator (over-defines mechanics).
- Not Cormac McCarthy's biblical cadence (too ornate, too recursive, no dialogue punctuation).
- Not Maas-style heat-forward romantasy (emotional implication over explicit description; we aim for Taylor-coded, not Maas-coded).
- Not Bardugo-style quippy banter (our dialogue has weight; jokes land but don't dominate).
- Not YA-contemporary chatty (voice-forward narration that names its own jokes).
- Not epic-fantasy omniscient (doesn't trust reader to assemble the world).
- Not literary-MFA interiority (all thought, no surface).
- Not empty-pretty (ornament without emotional or thematic work — the Morgenstern risk when Miller's precision isn't applied).

## 6. How to Use This File

- `context/WRITER_VOICE.md` in each project should **reference this file** and add 1–2 genre-specific model passages for that book's register.
- When in doubt, the moves here take precedence over per-project rules. Per-project files exist to *tune*, not to *replace*.
- If a per-project rule contradicts a rule here, the contradiction is the interesting question — write out why the genre demands the exception.
