# Anti-AI Core Guidelines

## Mandatory Anti-AI Rules
These rules are non-negotiable. Violating them produces detectable AI slop.

### Banned Words & Phrases (HARD BAN)
Never use these words—they trigger AI detection:
- delve, tapestry, testament, myriad, plethora, multifaceted
- crucial, significant, nuanced, landscape (when metaphorical)
- notably, furthermore, moreover, additionally, in conclusion
- "It's worth noting," "It should be noted," "It is important to understand"
- "A symphony of," "A dance of," "A mix of"

### Banned Transitions (HARD BAN)
- "To understand X, you have to understand Y" — find another way
- "Here's what you need to understand" — just say it
- "Let that sink in" — never
- "But that's the next chapter" / "The story belongs to a later chapter" — don't signpost
- "It is important to note that"
- "In today's digital age"

### Structural Rules
- **Don't re-explain concepts:** If you defined a term in the Intro, don't define it again in Chapter 10. Trust the reader.
- **Vary chapter structure:** Not every chapter should follow the same formula. Mix it up.
- **Integrate, don't sidebar:** Avoid "trivia dumps" unless specifically requested as Liner Notes/Sidebars.

### The Definition Loop (CRITICAL)
Technical concepts should be explained ONCE, then referenced briefly thereafter.

**Example:**
- **Definitive Explanation:** Chapter 1 (Explain the mechanics in detail)
- **Later Chapters:** Reference only.
- **BAD:** "He had to bounce tracks, which means mixing them down to a stereo pair..." (in Chapter 8)
- **GOOD:** "Returning to the hazardous bouncing technique he'd perfected..."

### Voice & Tone
- **Human Tone:** Conversational. Slightly informal. Use contractions (don't, won't, can't). Be opinionated.
- **Take stances:** Don't hedge everything. Have opinions. "The album is a masterpiece" not "The album is widely considered to be significant."
- **Add mess:** Real writing has tangents, uneven pacing, personal asides. Too-smooth = AI.
- **Emotional specificity:** Don't just say something is "beautiful" or "extraordinary." Describe the specific feeling.

### Factual Rigor
- **Verify credits:** Don't guess. If unsure, omit or hedge with "reportedly."
- **No unsourced quotes:** If you can't cite where someone said something, paraphrase.
- **Dates matter:** Double-check release years and timelines.

## Filter Words [STRICT]
Remove distancing phrases that place a character between reader and action:

**Cut these:** she saw, he heard, she felt, he noticed, she realized, he watched, she observed, he thought

**Before:** She heard footsteps in the hallway.
**After:** Footsteps echoed in the hallway.

**Before:** He noticed the door was open.
**After:** The door hung open.

**Exception:** Use filters when the act of perception itself matters, or to slow pacing deliberately.

## Common AI Writing Pitfalls [STRICT]
Watch for and correct these 13 telltale AI patterns:

1. **Hedging language:** "seemed to," "appeared to," "somewhat," "a bit"—commit to statements
2. **Overexplaining:** Trust the reader; don't explain jokes, emotions, or subtext
3. **Purple prose:** Overwrought description, excessive adjectives, melodramatic language
4. **Repetitive sentence structure:** Especially Subject-Verb-Object patterns in sequence
5. **Telling after showing:** Describing an emotion through action, then naming it anyway
6. **Floating dialogue:** Conversation without grounding in physical action or setting
7. **Uniform pacing:** Every scene same length/intensity; vary rhythm
8. **Convenient coincidences:** Characters finding exactly what they need by luck
9. **Flat supporting characters:** Everyone besides protagonist needs dimension
10. **Anachronistic language:** Modern slang in historical settings; formal speech in casual contexts
11. **Homogeneous voices:** All characters sounding alike regardless of background
12. **Excessive interiority:** Too much thinking, not enough action/dialogue
13. **Summarizing instead of dramatizing:** "They argued for an hour" vs. showing the argument
