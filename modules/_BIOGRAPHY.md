# Biography & Non-Fiction Writing

## The Biographer's Voice

A biography's voice should serve the subject and the project's purpose. Choose the appropriate mode below.

---

## Tone Mode A: Warm & Enthusiastic (Default)

**Best for:** Subjects you admire; fans writing for fans; celebratory but rigorous.

**Target Audience:** People who already love (or will come to love) the subject. They feel first, analyze second. They appreciate technique and respect eccentricity.

### Characteristics
- **Warm, enthusiastic, deeply human.** A fan writing for fans—with rigor.
- **Conversational but not sloppy.** You're telling a story to a friend.
- **Occasional wit, humor, or wry observation is welcome.**
- **Let your excitement show at transcendent moments.**

---

## Tone Mode B: Critical & Analytical

**Best for:** Complex, controversial, or morally ambiguous subjects; academic or investigative projects.

**Target Audience:** Readers seeking understanding, not validation. They want evidence and analysis.

### Characteristics
- **Objectivity first.** Present evidence, then interpretation.
- **Skeptical but fair.** Question claims, verify stories.
- **Judgment earned.** Criticisms supported by evidence, not just asserted.
- **Acknowledge strengths.** Even flawed subjects did things well.
- **Avoid contempt.** Analysis, not character assassination.

### Techniques
- Use conditional language when evidence is uncertain
- Present multiple interpretations of disputed events
- Let subjects' own words reveal character (for better or worse)
- Distinguish between contemporary judgment and historical context

---

## Tone Mode C: Dark & Complex

**Best for:** Troubled subjects; tragedy; stories where darkness is the point.

**Target Audience:** Readers who want the full truth, including the uncomfortable parts.

### Characteristics
- **Lean into contradictions.** The subject can be brilliant AND destructive.
- **Don't soften difficult material.** If it happened, it matters.
- **Let discomfort sit.** Don't rush to explain or excuse.
- **Empathy without endorsement.** Understand motivations; don't justify harm.
- **Use restraint.** The darkest moments need least embellishment.

### Techniques
- Let scenes speak for themselves (minimal editorializing)
- Slow down during difficult passages (give weight to pain)
- Include perspectives of those affected, not just the subject
- Avoid sensationalism—gravity, not spectacle

---

## Universal Principles (All Modes)

### Avoiding Hagiography
This is a biography, not a shrine. Great subjects are complex, contradictory, sometimes difficult.
- Show the struggles, the creative blocks, the commercial disappointments.
- Include tensions, disagreements, moments of doubt.
- Acknowledge when something didn't work, or when critics had a point.
- **Respect means honesty, not worship.** The reader trusts you to tell the truth.
- The subject is human. Let them be human.

## Writing Principles
1. **Sensory Specificity:** The crackle of tape, the smell of cigarette smoke, the humidity in the studio.
2. **Technical Integration:** Weave gear and production talk naturally—never dry lists.
3. **Human Moments:** Doubt before a take, exhaustion after a tour, silence when playback begins.
4. **Emotional Honesty:** If a work is devastating, say so. If a choice is audacious, let admiration show.
5. **Complexity:** Show contradictions. Genius and stubbornness. Warmth and distance. Success and failure.

## New Journalism Style
- Open with a scene or image, not exposition.
- End with forward momentum or resonant reflection.
- Vary paragraph length. A one-sentence paragraph hits different.
- **Voice:** Conversational. Slightly informal. Use contractions (don't, won't, can't). Be opinionated.
