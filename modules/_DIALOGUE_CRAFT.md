# Dialogue Craft

**Dialogue is action. Every line should do work: reveal character, advance plot, or create tension.**

---

## 1. THE SUBTEXT PRINCIPLE [STRICT]

Characters rarely say exactly what they mean. Dialogue is a tool for:
- **Persuading** — getting someone to do/believe something
- **Deflecting** — avoiding a topic or question
- **Wounding** — attacking without being direct
- **Seducing** — creating attraction or connection
- **Concealing** — hiding truth while appearing open

### What Characters Say vs. What They Mean

| Surface Line | Actual Meaning |
|-------------|----------------|
| "I'm fine." | I'm not fine, but I don't want to talk about it. |
| "Interesting choice." | I think you made a mistake. |
| "We should do this again sometime." | Polite dismissal; we won't. |
| "I didn't know you were coming." | Why are you here? |
| "That's one way to look at it." | You're wrong. |

### Writing Subtext

1. Know what the character actually wants in the scene
2. Know what they're trying to hide
3. Let them talk around the real issue
4. Show tension between words and body language
5. Let readers be smarter than the characters

**Example:**
> "You look tired," she said.
>
> "I'm fine."
>
> "I made coffee."
>
> "I don't want coffee."
>
> "It's on the counter if you change your mind."

*What's happening:* She's worried. He's shutting her out. Neither will name the thing between them.

## 2. INTERRUPTION PATTERNS

Real people interrupt. They talk over each other. They don't wait for complete thoughts.

### Em-Dash Interruption (Cut Off)
> "I was just trying to—"
>
> "Trying to what? Help? You call this helping?"

### Ellipsis Trailing (Fading Out)
> "I thought maybe we could..."
>
> She waited. He didn't finish.

### Self-Interruption
> "The thing is—no, forget it. It doesn't matter."

### Overlap (Indicated Through Context)
> "Listen, I need you to—"
>
> "I already know what you're going to say."
>
> "—just hear me out for once."

---

## 3. EVASION AND DEFLECTION

Characters dodge direct questions constantly. This creates tension.

### Common Deflection Techniques

**Answer a different question:**
> "Did you take the money?"
>
> "I've worked here for fifteen years."

**Redirect to the other person:**
> "Where were you last night?"
>
> "Why are you asking me that? Where were *you*?"

**Change the subject:**
> "We need to talk about what happened."
>
> "Have you eaten? I'm starving."

**Pretend not to understand:**
> "You know what I mean."
>
> "I really don't."

**Answer with a question:**
> "Are you seeing someone else?"
>
> "Would it matter if I was?"

## 4. VOICE DIFFERENTIATION [STRICT]

Every character should sound distinct. If you cover the dialogue tags, readers should still know who's speaking.

### Voice Components

| Element | How It Differs |
|---------|---------------|
| **Vocabulary** | Education, background, profession |
| **Sentence Length** | Terse vs. expansive |
| **Formality** | Contractions, slang, register |
| **Pet Phrases** | Repeated expressions unique to them |
| **What They Notice** | A cop notices exits; an artist notices light |
| **What They Avoid** | Topics, words, or admissions they won't make |

### Examples of Distinct Voices

**Character A (educated, formal, evasive):**
> "I'm not entirely certain that's an accurate characterization of events."

**Character B (working class, direct, confrontational):**
> "That's crap and you know it."

**Character C (nervous, overexplains, self-deprecating):**
> "No, I just—it's probably nothing, honestly. I'm probably making a big deal out of nothing. Forget I said anything."

### Voice Consistency Check
- Would this character use this word?
- Would they phrase it this way?
- Does this match their emotional state?
- Does this match their relationship with the listener?

## 5. PHYSICAL BLOCKING (Action Beats)

Dialogue needs physical grounding. Characters exist in space and interact with their environment.

### Action Beats vs. Dialogue Tags

**Dialogue tag (invisible):**
> "I can't do this anymore," she said.

**Action beat (visible):**
> "I can't do this anymore." She set the ring on the table.

### Using Action Beats

- **Ground the scene:** Show where characters are, what they're doing
- **Show emotion:** Actions reveal what words hide
- **Control pacing:** Beats slow down tense moments
- **Avoid "talking heads":** Readers need visual anchors

**Example with beats:**
> Marcus set down his fork. "You told her."
>
> "I had to."
>
> He pushed back from the table. The chair scraped loud against the floor.
>
> "You *had* to." He wasn't looking at her now. "That's your defense."

### When NOT to Use Beats

- Rapid-fire exchanges (let them breathe)
- When you've established the scene
- When pacing should accelerate

**Clean rapid exchange:**
> "You lied."
>
> "Everyone lies."
>
> "Not to me."
>
> "Especially to you."

## 6. DIALOGUE TAGS [STRICT]

### Default: "Said" and "Asked"

These are invisible. They don't distract. Use them.

### Avoid These Tags
- exclaimed, proclaimed, intoned, queried, responded, retorted
- hissed (unless actually hissing)
- laughed, sighed, smiled (you can't "laugh" a sentence)

### Acceptable Alternatives (Sparingly)
- whispered, shouted, muttered (when volume matters)
- admitted, lied (when the meta-information matters)

### Better Than Tags: Action Beats

Instead of:
> "Get out," she said angrily.

Write:
> "Get out." She threw his jacket at the door.

## 7. DIALECT AND ACCENT [FLEXIBLE]

### The Rule
Suggest dialect through word choice and rhythm, not phonetic spelling.

### Bad (Phonetic)
> "Ah dinnae ken whit ye mean."

### Good (Suggested)
> "I don't know what you mean." His accent thickened the vowels.

### Techniques for Dialect

- **Word choice:** "y'all," "reckon," "proper" (British), "wee"
- **Sentence structure:** Different syntax patterns
- **What they don't say:** Dropped words, implied meanings
- **Occasional phonetic touch:** One word, not every word

**Example:**
> "That dog ain't worth the trouble." He squinted at the horizon. "Never was."

---

## 8. EXPOSITION THROUGH DIALOGUE

### The "As You Know, Bob" Trap [STRICT]

Characters should never explain things to each other that they both already know.

**Bad:**
> "As you know, Bob, the reactor core has been unstable since the accident three years ago."

**Good:**
> "The core's acting up again."
>
> "Like '21?"
>
> "Worse."

### Legitimate Exposition Dialogue

- Character genuinely doesn't know
- Character is testing if the other knows
- Character is reminding/emphasizing for emotional effect
- Character is explaining to a third party present

---

## 9. SILENCE AND NON-RESPONSE

Sometimes the most powerful dialogue is what isn't said.

### Ways to Show Silence

> He didn't answer. He didn't have to.

> She asked him twice. He kept walking.

> "Well?"
>
> Nothing. Just the sound of the rain.

### Non-Verbal Responses

> "Did you love her?"
>
> He looked at the photograph for a long time.

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Every line does work (reveals character, advances plot, creates tension)
- [ ] Subtext present (characters rarely say exactly what they mean)
- [ ] Voices distinct (could identify speaker without tags)
- [ ] Scene grounded (action beats, physical space)
- [ ] No "As you know, Bob" exposition
- [ ] Tags invisible (said/asked default)
- [ ] Dialect suggested, not spelled out
