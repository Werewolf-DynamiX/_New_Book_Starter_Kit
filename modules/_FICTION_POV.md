# Fiction POV & Dialogue

## POV Core Rule [STRICT]
**No head-hopping:** Do not switch POV within a scene. One perspective per scene minimum.

---

## First Person POV Rules

### The Fundamentals
- Stay locked in the narrator's head—she can't know what others think, only observe and interpret.
- Present tense means immediacy: "I duck" not "I ducked".
- Filter words are mostly unnecessary: "I see him frown" → "He frowns".
- She can be wrong about things. Let her be wrong sometimes.
- Her observations are colored by mood—describe the world through her emotional state.

### POV Traps to Avoid
- No head-hopping (she can't know he's thinking about his tragic backstory).
- No "I feel myself smile"—just "I smile".
- No "I can't help but notice"—she just notices.
- Avoid "I find myself"—weak and passive.
- Don't describe her own face unless she's looking in a mirror (and even then, sparingly).

### What She CAN Do
- Misread situations based on her biases.
- Notice body language and draw conclusions (right or wrong).
- Have her thoughts interrupted mid-sentence by action.
- Contradict herself when emotions are high.
- Fixate on weird details when stressed (this is human).

## Internal Monologue Patterns
Her thoughts should feel like actual thoughts:
- **Interruptions:** "Maybe he's—no. No, I'm not doing this."
- **Self-corrections:** "He's kind. Or he wants me to think he's kind."
- **Tangents that get yanked back:** "The scar curves from his jaw to his—focus."
- **Questions she doesn't answer:** "What does he want from me?"
- **Contradictions:** "I hate him. Gods, I hate how much I want him to stay."

## Dialogue Patterns
- **Interruption:** People interrupt each other.
- **Evasion:** People don't always answer the question asked.
- **Trailing off:** "I just thought maybe we could—"
- **Fillers:** People say "um," "well," "I mean" (sparingly).
- **Repetition:** People repeat themselves when emotional: "I trusted you. I *trusted* you."
- **Tags:** Not every line needs a dialogue tag or action beat—let exchanges breathe.

## Third Person Limited POV
- Close to one character's perspective per scene
- Can access thoughts but filtered through that character's perception
- More flexibility than first person; less intimacy
- Best for: most commercial fiction, multi-POV stories

## Third Person Omniscient POV
- Narrator is a distinct entity with its own voice
- Can know anything but must be consistent in what it reveals
- Requires clear establishment of narrator's personality
- Best for: epic scope, satirical tone, classic literary style

---

## Unreliable Narrators [FLEXIBLE]
When using an unreliable narrator:
- The unreliability must serve story purpose (trauma, self-protection, delusion)
- The narrator believes their own version; they are the hero of their story
- Plant subtle clues that observant readers can catch
- Never lie directly to the reader as author—filter lies through character perception
- The truth should recontextualize, not invalidate, earlier scenes

---

## Dialogue Mechanics [STRICT]

### Formatting
- New speaker = new paragraph, always
- Action beats can replace dialogue tags: "Stop it." She slammed the book shut.
- Use "said" as default tag; it's invisible. Avoid: exclaimed, proclaimed, intoned, queried

### Avoid
- "As you know, Bob" exposition (characters telling each other known information)
- Excessive name usage in dialogue (people rarely use names in real conversation)
- On-the-nose dialogue where characters state exactly what they feel

### Subtext Principle
Characters use dialogue to achieve goals—persuade, deflect, wound, seduce, conceal. They rarely say exactly what they mean.
