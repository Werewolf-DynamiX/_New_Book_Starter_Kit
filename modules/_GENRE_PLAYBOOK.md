# Genre-Specific Playbook

Each genre has its own contract with readers. Know the expectations, then decide which to honor and which to subvert.

---

## 1. Thriller / Suspense

**Core Promise:** Relentless tension. Reader should feel unsafe.

### Essential Elements
- **Open with threat:** Action, danger, or ominous setup in first scene
- **Ticking clock:** Something must happen by a deadline
- **Credible antagonist:** Equal or superior to protagonist in capability
- **Fair clues:** Readers should be able to piece things together
- **Escalating stakes:** Each act raises the cost of failure

### Structure
1. **Setup:** Establish normal world, hint at coming threat
2. **Inciting incident:** Protagonist pulled into danger
3. **Rising danger:** Complications, false leads, near-misses
4. **Dark moment:** All seems lost; antagonist has upper hand
5. **Climax:** Direct confrontation, highest stakes
6. **Resolution:** Threat neutralized, costs revealed

### Common Pitfalls
- Protagonist too reactive (must drive action eventually)
- Villain's plan makes no sense under scrutiny
- Too many coincidences rescue protagonist
- Pacing that lets reader relax
- Deus ex machina resolution

### Thriller Subgenres
| Subgenre | Focus | Key Element |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Psychological | Mind games | Unreliable narrator, gaslighting |
| Action | Physical danger | Set pieces, chase sequences |
| Political | Power, conspiracy | Institutional threat |
| Domestic | Family, home | Trapped with threat |

## 2. Mystery

**Core Promise:** A puzzle the reader can solve alongside the detective.

### Essential Elements
- **Play fair:** All clues available to reader before solution
- **Compelling victim:** Make readers care who died (or what was stolen)
- **Plausible red herrings:** Misdirection that makes sense in retrospect
- **Earned solution:** Detective solves through effort, not luck
- **Satisfying reveal:** Solution recontextualizes earlier scenes

### Structure
1. **Discovery:** Crime/mystery introduced
2. **Investigation:** Gather clues, interview suspects
3. **Complications:** False leads, new information, rising danger
4. **Revelation:** Key piece clicks into place
5. **Confrontation:** Detective faces perpetrator
6. **Resolution:** Explanation and consequences

### The "Fair Play" Rules [STRICT]
- No withheld clues revealed only in the solution
- Murderer must be introduced early (not final 20%)
- No supernatural solutions in realistic mysteries
- Detective can't withhold thoughts from reader (in 1st person)
- No twin/double without prior establishment

### Common Pitfalls
- Clues too obvious (no puzzle)
- Clues too obscure (reader feels cheated)
- Detective is insufferable genius with no flaws
- Investigation feels like interviews in sequence
- Solution requires knowledge reader couldn't have

## 3. Fantasy / Science Fiction

**Core Promise:** A world with consistent internal rules that create story.

### Essential Elements
- **Early establishment:** Rules of magic/tech introduced in Act 1
- **Consistent application:** Rules don't bend for plot convenience
- **Integration through action:** Worldbuilding revealed through story, not exposition
- **Clear costs/limits:** Magic/tech that can do anything is boring
- **Gradual terminology:** Made-up words introduced with context

### Worldbuilding Integration

**Show, don't info-dump:**
| Bad | Good |
|-----|------|
| Long explanation of magic system | Character uses magic, experiences cost |
| History lecture | Character references event naturally |
| Map description | Character travels, discovers |

### Magic/Technology Rules [STRICT]
- **Establish limits:** What can't it do?
- **Define costs:** What does using it require?
- **Stay consistent:** Same spell/tech works the same way
- **Avoid "just in time" abilities:** No new powers for convenience

### Common Pitfalls
- Worldbuilding overwhelms story
- Magic solves every problem
- Invented terminology dense to the point of unreadable
- Earth-centric assumptions in alien worlds
- Technology as magic (no explanation or limits)

## 4. Literary Fiction

**Core Promise:** Voice, depth, and emotional truth over plot velocity.

### Essential Elements
- **Distinctive voice:** Style is content
- **Thematic resonance:** Story explores ideas, not just events
- **Character interiority:** Rich inner life of protagonist
- **Emotional truth:** Authentic human experience
- **Ambiguity accepted:** Not all questions need answers

### Priorities (In Order)
1. Voice and style
2. Character depth
3. Thematic coherence
4. Emotional authenticity
5. Plot (serves above, doesn't drive)

### Structure Flexibility
- Non-linear timelines acceptable
- Experimental form serves meaning
- Quiet moments can be climactic
- Resolution can be internal, not external

### Common Pitfalls
- All style, no substance
- So ambiguous it's meaningless
- Characters who exist only to represent ideas
- Contempt for plot (story still needs forward motion)
- Pretentious opacity mistaken for depth

## 5. Historical Fiction

**Core Promise:** Immersion in a different time, with emotional relevance to now.

### Essential Elements
- **Deep research, light wear:** Know more than you show
- **Period-appropriate language:** Accessible but not jarringly modern
- **Characters of their time:** Values and assumptions appropriate to era
- **Real events as backdrop:** History supports story, rarely drives it
- **Sensory authenticity:** What did the era smell, sound, feel like?

### Language Rules
| Avoid | Consider Instead |
|-------|-----------------|
| Modern slang | Period-neutral phrasing |
| Thee/thou (usually) | Slightly formal register |
| Anachronistic concepts | Era-appropriate worldview |

### Research Integration
- Details that reveal character, not just era
- Physical sensations over Wikipedia facts
- Specific over generic (the actual food, not "they ate")

### Common Pitfalls
- Modern values imposed on historical characters
- Research overwhelming story
- Protagonists who are "ahead of their time" on everything
- Famous historical figures as cardboard props
- Sanitized history (clean streets, perfect teeth)

## 7. Horror

**Core Promise:** Provoke fear, dread, or revulsion. Make the reader uncomfortable.

### The Three Flavors
| Type | Effect | Techniques |
|------|--------|------------|
| **Terror** | Anticipation of threat | Darkness, unknown, suspense |
| **Horror** | Confrontation with threat | The reveal, the monster, the truth |
| **Revulsion** | Physical disgust | Body horror, decay, wrongness |

### Essential Elements
- **Atmosphere:** Dread before the scare
- **The Uncanny:** Familiar made wrong
- **Escalation:** Each scare bigger than last
- **Character investment:** Fear for characters, not just with them
- **Cost:** Horror has consequences; safety isn't guaranteed

### Pacing Fear
1. **Establish normalcy:** Reader needs baseline to disturb
2. **First hint:** Something wrong, dismissable
3. **Confirmation:** It's real, it's here
4. **Escalation:** Each encounter worse
5. **Dark night of the soul:** All seems lost
6. **Climax:** Full confrontation with horror
7. **Resolution:** Survival (or not), lasting cost

### Monster Reveal Rules
- **Delay gratification:** Imagination is scarier than description
- **Partial reveals first:** Glimpses before full reveal
- **Sound before sight:** Let readers hear what's coming
- **The reveal changes things:** Full monster = different story mode

### Common Pitfalls
- Too much too soon (no escalation left)
- Gore substituting for dread
- Protagonist makes stupid choices for plot
- Monster loses mystery (becomes just an obstacle)
- Safe ending undermines horror (everyone's fine!)

## 8. Comedy / Humor

**Core Promise:** Make the reader laugh (or at least smile).

### Fundamentals
- **Timing is everything:** The pause, the delay, the unexpected beat
- **Specificity kills:** Specific details are funnier than vague ones
- **Character-driven > joke-driven:** Humor from who they are, not random gags
- **The audience must feel safe:** Comedy requires distance from real pain

### The Rule of Three
1. Establish pattern
2. Confirm pattern
3. Subvert pattern

**Example:** "He was tall, dark, and—three ferrets in a trench coat."

### Benign Violation Theory
Comedy happens when something is:
- Wrong/unexpected/threatening (violation)
- But simultaneously okay/safe/acceptable (benign)

**Both required.** Pure violation = horror. Pure benign = boring.

### Comedic Techniques
| Technique | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Incongruity** | Mismatch between expectation and reality |
| **Escalation** | Taking premise to absurd extremes |
| **Callback** | Referencing earlier joke in new context |
| **Understatement** | Downplaying something enormous |
| **Bathos** | Undercutting serious moment with mundane |

### Common Pitfalls
- Trying too hard (desperation shows)
- Punching down (mean isn't funny)
- Explaining the joke
- Repeating the same joke (diminishing returns)
- Breaking character for a gag
- Comedy that doesn't serve story

## 9. Middle Grade (Ages 8-12)

**Core Promise:** Adventure, agency, and hope. The world is bigger than they knew.

### Essential Elements
- **Protagonist agency:** They solve the problem, not rescued adults
- **Adults in support:** Present but not controlling
- **Age-appropriate complexity:** Clear stakes, clear morality (with nuance)
- **Hope required:** The world can be made better
- **Discovery:** Learning something about self/world

### Content Guidelines
| Element | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| **Violence** | Present but not graphic; consequences shown |
| **Death** | Possible but handled with weight |
| **Romance** | Crushes, awkwardness—no physical intimacy |
| **Language** | No profanity; mild euphemisms okay |
| **Scary content** | Suspense yes; trauma-inducing no |

### Voice
- First person or tight third common
- Voice reflects age (not too sophisticated, not condescending)
- Humor welcome (kids love funny)
- Emotional honesty (kids know when you're faking)

### Structure
- Clear goals and obstacles
- Faster pacing than adult fiction
- Chapter breaks at hooks
- Shorter chapters (often 8-15 pages)
- Satisfying resolution (can set up sequels)

### Common Pitfalls
- Adults constantly saving the day
- Protagonist too passive
- Moralizing/preaching
- Condescending voice
- Content too mature or too babyish
- Everything wrapped up too neatly (kids appreciate complexity)

## 10. Young Adult (Ages 14-18)

**Core Promise:** Identity, intensity, and finding your place in the world.

### Essential Elements
- **Identity questions:** Who am I? Where do I belong?
- **First experiences:** First love, first independence, first betrayal
- **Emotional intensity:** Big feelings, treated as valid
- **Coming of age:** Character transforms through story
- **Stakes that matter:** Consequences feel world-ending (to them)

### Content Guidelines
- More flexibility than MG
- Violence, death, romance can be present
- Sexual content varies (open door to fade to black)
- Profanity as appropriate to character
- Heavy topics handled with care

### Voice Requirements
- Protagonist 14-18 (reader typically reads up)
- Authentic teen voice (not adult impersonating teen)
- Emotional honesty paramount
- Adults can be flawed/absent/antagonistic

## 11. Memoir (Narrative Nonfiction)

**Core Promise:** Transform lived experience into universal meaning. The "I" becomes an "us."

### How Memoir Differs from Autobiography
| Autobiography | Memoir |
|---------------|--------|
| Comprehensive life story | Focused slice of life |
| Chronological | Thematic structure |
| "What happened" | "What it meant" |
| External events | Internal transformation |

### Essential Elements
- **Focused scope:** One period, theme, or transformation—not whole life
- **Emotional arc:** The narrator changes through the telling
- **Universal resonance:** Personal story illuminates larger truth
- **Honest self-portrayal:** Narrator has flaws, makes mistakes, gets things wrong
- **Scene-based:** Show moments; don't just summarize years

### The Memoir Narrator
- **Then-self vs. Now-self:** Distinguish between who you were and who you are
- **Unreliable by nature:** Memory is reconstruction, not recording
- **Honest about uncertainty:** "I don't remember if..." is allowed
- **Reflection without preaching:** Let meaning emerge; don't moralize

### Structure Options
| Structure | Best For |
|-----------|----------|
| **Chronological** | Coming-of-age, recovery narratives |
| **Thematic** | Essays, braided narratives |
| **Frame narrative** | Present triggering past memories |
| **Reverse chronological** | Mystery of self, trauma excavation |

### Scene Construction (The Hybrid Challenge)
Memoir uses fiction techniques but nonfiction ethics:

| Fiction Technique | Memoir Adaptation |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Dialogue | Reconstructed from memory; essence over exact words |
| Sensory detail | What you actually remember; acknowledge gaps |
| Scene structure | Goal/Obstacle/Outcome for real moments |
| Character development | Real people; show complexity without libel |

### Ethical Boundaries [STRICT]
- **No composite characters** (combine two people into one)
- **No invented scenes** (create moments that didn't happen)
- **No false dialogue** (put words in mouths that change meaning)
- **Acknowledge uncertainty** when memory is unclear
- **Protect the innocent** (name changes, obscured details for non-public figures)

### Common Pitfalls
- All reflection, no scene (tell us what happened)
- All scene, no reflection (tell us what it means)
- Self-aggrandizement (narrator always right/heroic)
- Score-settling disguised as truth-telling
- Present-day wisdom attributed to past self
- Treating memory as perfect recording

## Quick Reference: Genre at a Glance

| Genre | Core Engine | Must Have | Must Avoid |
|-------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Thriller | Tension | Ticking clock | Letting reader relax |
| Mystery | Puzzle | Fair clues | Cheating |
| Fantasy/SF | Wonder | Consistent rules | Convenient magic |
| Literary | Voice | Depth | Empty style |
| Historical | Immersion | Research (worn light) | Anachronism |
| Horror | Fear | Escalation | Too much too soon |
| Comedy | Laughter | Timing | Explaining jokes |
| Middle Grade | Adventure | Protagonist agency | Adults saving day |
| YA | Identity | Emotional truth | Condescension |
| Memoir | Meaning | Emotional honesty | Invention |
