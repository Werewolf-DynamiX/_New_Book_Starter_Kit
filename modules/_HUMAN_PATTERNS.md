# Human Writing Patterns

## Sentence Rhythm (Burstiness)
**Vary sentence length aggressively. This is non-negotiable.**

- Mix short punches (2-6 words) with medium flow (8-15 words) and occasional long unspooling thoughts (18-30 words).
- Never write more than two sentences of similar length back-to-back.
- After a long sentence, go short. After several short ones, let one breathe.
- One-word sentences are legal. Use them.
- Sentence fragments too. Especially in high tension.

**Example of GOOD rhythm:**
> The throne room falls silent. I count three breaths—mine loud in my ears, ragged and too fast—before the king speaks. One word. Just my name. And somehow that's worse than if he'd ordered my execution on the spot, because now I have to stand here, have to meet his eyes, have to pretend my knees aren't shaking beneath this ridiculous gown.

**Example of BAD rhythm (too uniform):**
> The throne room falls silent as I enter. I count my breaths while waiting for the king to speak. He says my name in a cold voice. I feel terrified but try to hide it. My knees shake beneath my gown.

## Paragraph Variation
- Vary paragraph length: some single sentences, some 4-5 sentences.
- Not every paragraph needs a topic sentence.
- Sometimes start mid-thought.
- One-line paragraphs hit hard. Use them for impact.
- Don't let every paragraph be 3 sentences. That's a pattern. Break it.

## Word Choice (Perplexity)
- Avoid the most predictable word—but don't strain for obscurity.
- Specific beats generic: "oak" not "tree," "slash" not "cut," "chartreuse" not "green" (when it matters).
- The narrator's voice should have pet words and phrases she returns to (but not every page).
- Occasional informal constructions: "gonna," "kinda," "way too".
- Let grammar bend for voice: "Which, okay, fair" is fine in her internal monologue.

## Structural Tells to Avoid
- Don't use three examples when one lands harder.
- Not every scene needs beginning/middle/end—some can start late, end early.
- Avoid the pattern of: action, reaction, internal thought, repeat.
- Don't mirror sentence structures in adjacent sentences.
- Skip the obvious transition—readers can follow jumps.

## Show vs. Tell

**Default to showing** for: emotional moments, important scenes, character-revealing actions

**Telling is acceptable** for: transitions, conveying time passage, summarizing less important events, varying rhythm

### Showing Technique
Replace emotion labels with physical sensation and action:
- **Bad:** "She was angry."
- **Good:** "Her jaw tightened. She set down the glass with excessive care."

### Sentence Rhythm for Effect [FLEXIBLE]
- **Short sentences:** tension, impact, action, revelation
- **Long sentences:** description, introspection, flowing action, dreamlike states
- **Fragments:** emphasis, internal reaction, staccato effect

### Rhythm Traps to Avoid
- Multiple sentences starting with character name/pronoun
- "THUD THUD THUD" rhythm of identical Subject-Verb patterns
- Overuse of participial phrases ("Walking to the door, she...")
