# KDP Book Review Framework
## Master Template for Self-Publishing Quality Assurance

> **Purpose:** Catch structural problems BEFORE they're baked in. Ensure every book is market-ready, emotionally resonant, and sells well on Amazon KDP.

> **Philosophy:** Reviews must FIND problems, not validate choices. If a review session doesn't identify at least 3 things to improve, the review wasn't honest enough.

## 1.2 Worldbuilding Hook Test

### The One-Sentence Test
*Describe your setting in ONE memorable sentence that makes someone want to visit:*

> _______________________________________________

**Evaluation:**
- [ ] Does it sound unique, or could it describe any village/city/world?
- [ ] Does it contain a sensory detail?
- [ ] Does it hint at conflict or intrigue?

**BAD EXAMPLES:**
- "A cozy village where everyone knows each other" (generic)
- "A magical kingdom with secrets" (vague)

**GOOD EXAMPLES:**
- "A village where the morning fog carries whispers of the dead, and the baker charges extra for silence"
- "A bookshop built into a cliff face, where the tide determines which shelves you can reach"

### Setting Specificity Checklist
- [ ] **Economy:** How do people make money here? What's the main trade?
- [ ] **History Scar:** What past event left a mark everyone still talks about?
- [ ] **Local Quirk:** What's one weird tradition or rule unique to this place?
- [ ] **Sensory Signature:** What does this place smell/sound/feel like?

## 1.3 Crisis Source Validation

### The Conversation Test
*For your planned Act 3 crisis, answer honestly:*

**What is the crisis?** _______________

**Could this be resolved with a single honest conversation?**
- [ ] YES → **STOP. This is manufactured conflict. Redesign.**
- [ ] NO → Proceed, but explain why not: _______________

### Crisis Origin Checklist
- [ ] Crisis emerges from CHARACTER (their flaw, their fear, their choice)
- [ ] Crisis emerges from WORLD (external forces beyond their control)
- [ ] Crisis was seeded earlier in the story (not invented for Act 3)
- [ ] Resolution requires CHARACTER GROWTH, not just information

**RED FLAGS for Manufactured Crisis:**
- Character runs away/hides instead of talking
- Misunderstanding that one sentence could fix
- Character acts out-of-character to create drama
- Crisis exists because "Act 3 needs conflict"

- Bad timing
- Shyness
- "We're too different" (without concrete stakes)

**REAL OBSTACLES (at least ONE required):**
- [ ] External circumstance that physically separates them
- [ ] Conflicting loyalties (family, duty, prior commitment)
- [ ] One character must sacrifice something significant to be together
- [ ] Past trauma that makes intimacy genuinely dangerous
- [ ] Power imbalance that must be addressed
- [ ] Goals that are mutually exclusive

# PHASE 2: MID-DRAFT CHECKPOINTS
*Complete at 25%, 50%, and 75% through draft*

## 2.1 Beat Audit (25% Mark)

- [ ] Hook delivers a promise in first 3 pages
- [ ] Protagonist's wound is demonstrated, not explained
- [ ] Setting feels specific and lived-in
- [ ] At least one supporting character has shown independent life

## 2.2 Momentum Check (50% Mark)

- [ ] Romance/plot has escalated (not just repeated beats)
- [ ] Obstacles have gotten harder, not easier
- [ ] Supporting cast has had at least one scene focused on them
- [ ] Worldbuilding details have paid off (not just decoration)

## 2.3 Crisis Validation (75% Mark)

- [ ] Run the Conversation Test again on your current crisis plan
- [ ] Verify crisis emerges from seeds planted earlier
- [ ] Confirm resolution requires growth, not just luck

# PHASE 5: LESSONS LEARNED CHECKPOINTS
*Explicit verification against known failure modes*

## Checkpoint 1: Manufactured Crisis
**Lesson:** We created a crisis because "Act 3 needed conflict" - not from character.

- [ ] Crisis passes the Conversation Test
- [ ] Crisis emerges from character flaw or wound
- [ ] Crisis was foreshadowed earlier
- [ ] Resolution requires internal growth

**Verdict:** PASS / FAIL / NEEDS REVISION

---

## Checkpoint 2: Underwritten Love Interest
**Lesson:** Love interest had no goals or arc independent of protagonist.

- [ ] LI has stated independent goal (written in Section 1.1)
- [ ] LI pursues this goal in at least 2 scenes
- [ ] LI would be interesting even without romance
- [ ] LI has their own moment of growth/change

**Verdict:** PASS / FAIL / NEEDS REVISION

---

## Checkpoint 3: Thin Worldbuilding
**Lesson:** Setting was generic and unmemorable.

- [ ] Setting passes One-Sentence Test
- [ ] Economy, history scar, local quirk, and sensory signature defined
- [ ] Setting details affect plot (not just backdrop)
- [ ] Reader could describe this world to a friend

**Verdict:** PASS / FAIL / NEEDS REVISION

---

## Checkpoint 4: Flat Supporting Cast
**Lesson:** Supporting characters were mentioned but never developed.

- [ ] 3-4 supporting characters identified in table
- [ ] Each has distinct personality AND personal want
- [ ] Each has at least one scene pursuing their want
- [ ] At least one supporting character surprises the reader

**Verdict:** PASS / FAIL / NEEDS REVISION

---

## Checkpoint 5: Romance Without Real Obstacles
**Lesson:** Only obstacle was mutual reluctance.

- [ ] At least one REAL obstacle identified (from approved list)
- [ ] Obstacle creates genuine tension (not easily dismissed)
- [ ] Obstacle requires sacrifice or growth to overcome
- [ ] Obstacle is resolved, not ignored

**Verdict:** PASS / FAIL / NEEDS REVISION

# PHASE 6: HONEST FEEDBACK PROTOCOL
*How to ensure reviews actually find problems*

## 6.1 The Minimum Problem Rule
**Every review session must identify at least 3 problems.**

If you can't find 3 problems, you're not looking hard enough. Use these prompts:

1. "What would make a reader put this down?"
2. "What would a harsh Goodreads review say?"
3. "If I had to cut 20%, what would be the least missed?"
4. "Where did I get bored writing this?"
5. "What am I defending instead of fixing?"

---

## 6.2 The Devil's Advocate Pass
**Assign someone (or a role) to argue AGAINST the book.**

Their job:
- Find the weakest subplot and argue for cutting it
- Identify the least interesting character and justify removing them
- Point out where pacing lags
- Question every "emotional moment" - is it earned?

---

## 6.3 The Stranger Test
**Imagine someone who doesn't know you reads this.**

They don't know:
- What you intended
- Why you made certain choices
- Your personal connection to the material
- How hard you worked

**Would they:**
- [ ] Finish the book?
- [ ] Recommend it?
- [ ] Pay full price?
- [ ] Read the sequel?

If any answer is "probably not," that's a problem to address.

---

## 6.4 The Revision Priority Matrix

After collecting all feedback, categorize:

| Priority | Definition | Action |
|----------|------------|--------|
| **Critical** | Breaks the book (plot hole, flat protagonist) | Must fix before publication |
| **Important** | Weakens the book (thin scenes, pacing issues) | Should fix if time allows |
| **Nice-to-Have** | Would improve but not essential | Fix in future editions |
| **Taste** | Subjective preference, not quality issue | Consider but don't stress |
