# Narrative Voice & POV

## Core Rule [STRICT]
**No head-hopping:** Do not switch POV within a scene. One perspective per scene minimum.

---

## First Person POV

### First Person Present Tense

The most intimate and immediate POV. Reader experiences events as they happen.

**Fundamentals:**
- Stay locked in the narrator's head—they can't know what others think, only observe and interpret
- Present tense means immediacy: "I duck" not "I ducked"
- Filter words are mostly unnecessary: "I see him frown" → "He frowns"
- The narrator can be wrong. Let them be wrong sometimes
- Their observations are colored by mood—describe the world through their emotional state

**Example (female narrator):**
> The throne room falls silent. I count three breaths—mine loud in my ears, ragged and too fast—before the king speaks. One word. Just my name.

**Example (male narrator):**
> The conference room goes quiet. I watch the CEO's jaw tighten, and I know before he opens his mouth that this pitch is dead. Three months of work. Gone in the time it takes him to shake his head.

**POV Traps to Avoid:**
- No "I feel myself smile"—just "I smile"
- No "I can't help but notice"—they just notice
- Avoid "I find myself"—weak and passive
- Don't describe their own face unless looking in a mirror (and even then, sparingly)

**What the Narrator CAN Do:**
- Misread situations based on their biases
- Notice body language and draw conclusions (right or wrong)
- Have thoughts interrupted mid-sentence by action
- Contradict themselves when emotions are high
- Fixate on weird details when stressed (this is human)

---

### First Person Past Tense

More reflective than present. The narrator has survived to tell the tale.

**Key Differences from Present:**
- Narrator knows how things turn out (can hint, foreshadow, withhold)
- "I walked" not "I walk"
- Slight distance allows for more reflection
- Can comment on past self with hindsight

**Example (female narrator):**
> I didn't know then what that silence meant. Looking back, I can see the moment everything shifted—the way Marcus's hand stopped mid-gesture, the way Elena's smile hardened into something else entirely.

**Example (male narrator):**
> That was the summer I learned to lie. Not the clumsy half-truths of childhood, but the clean, surgical deceptions that would define the next decade of my life.

## Third Person POV

### Third Person Limited

Close to one character's perspective per scene. Most common in commercial fiction.

**Characteristics:**
- Can access thoughts, but filtered through that character's perception
- More flexibility than first person; less intimacy
- Still bound by what the POV character knows and notices
- Best for: multi-POV stories, commercial fiction, genre fiction

**Example (following "she"):**
> Maya pressed her back against the wall, heart slamming against her ribs. The footsteps paused. She could hear breathing on the other side of the door—slow, patient. Whoever it was, they knew she was there.

**Example (following "he"):**
> Jack watched the interrogation through one-way glass, his coffee going cold in his hand. Something was off. The suspect's story was too clean, too rehearsed. Twenty years of detective work had taught him to recognize the texture of a practiced lie.

**Third Limited Traps:**
- Don't slip into other characters' thoughts
- Don't reveal information the POV character couldn't know
- Keep emotional distance consistent within scenes

---

### Third Person Omniscient

The narrator is a distinct entity with its own voice. Knows everything, sees everything.

**Characteristics:**
- Can reveal any character's thoughts (but must be consistent about when)
- Narrator personality must be established early
- Requires clear establishment of narrator's relationship to story
- Best for: epic scope, satirical tone, classic literary style

**Example:**
> What neither of them knew—what neither could have guessed—was that the letter sitting unopened on Margaret's desk would change everything. James would spend the next thirty years wondering what might have happened if he'd arrived ten minutes earlier. Margaret would spend them wondering why he hadn't.

**Omniscient Pitfalls:**
- Inconsistent revelation (why share some thoughts but not others?)
- Losing intimacy by staying too far above the action
- Head-hopping disguised as omniscience

## Internal Monologue Patterns

Thoughts should feel like actual thoughts, not composed prose:

- **Interruptions:** "Maybe he's—no. No, I'm not doing this."
- **Self-corrections:** "He's kind. Or he wants me to think he's kind."
- **Tangents yanked back:** "The scar curves from his jaw to his—focus."
- **Questions left unanswered:** "What does he want from me?"
- **Contradictions:** "I hate him. Gods, I hate how much I want him to stay."

**Past Tense Variant:**
- **Reflection:** "I should have seen it coming. The signs were all there."
- **Hindsight:** "That was my first mistake. It wouldn't be my last."

---

## Unreliable Narrators [FLEXIBLE]

When using an unreliable narrator:
- The unreliability must serve story purpose (trauma, self-protection, delusion)
- The narrator believes their own version; they are the hero of their story
- Plant subtle clues that observant readers can catch
- Never lie directly to the reader as author—filter lies through character perception
- The truth should recontextualize, not invalidate, earlier scenes

---

## POV Selection Guide

| POV | Best For | Trade-offs |
|-----|----------|------------|
| **1st Present** | Intimacy, immediacy, unreliable narrators, thriller/romance | Can feel claustrophobic; limited scope |
| **1st Past** | Memoir tone, reflection, survival stories | Less immediate; narrator knows outcome |
| **3rd Limited** | Multi-POV, commercial fiction, mysteries | Less intimate than 1st; more than omniscient |
| **3rd Omniscient** | Epic scope, satire, classic literary | Requires strong narrator voice; risks distance |

## Dialogue Mechanics [STRICT]

**For comprehensive dialogue guidance, see `_DIALOGUE_CRAFT.md`.**

### Quick Reference

**Formatting:**
- New speaker = new paragraph, always
- Action beats can replace dialogue tags: "Stop it." She slammed the book shut.
- Use "said" as default tag; it's invisible. Avoid: exclaimed, proclaimed, intoned, queried

**Avoid:**
- "As you know, Bob" exposition (characters telling each other known information)
- Excessive name usage in dialogue (people rarely use names in real conversation)
- On-the-nose dialogue where characters state exactly what they feel

**Subtext Principle:**
Characters use dialogue to achieve goals—persuade, deflect, wound, seduce, conceal. They rarely say exactly what they mean.
