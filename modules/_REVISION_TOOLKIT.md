# Revision Toolkit

**When you identify a problem, this guide tells you how to fix it.**

Each entry follows: **Symptom → Diagnosis → Fix**

---

## SCENE-LEVEL PROBLEMS

### "The Scene Feels Flat"

**Symptom:** Scene exists but nothing seems to happen. Reader could skip it.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- What does the POV character want in this scene?
- What's stopping them from getting it?
- What changes by the end?

**Fix:**
1. Add a concrete goal (even if small)
2. Add an obstacle (person, circumstance, internal conflict)
3. Ensure the outcome matters (success, failure, or complication)

If the scene still feels flat after these fixes, the scene may be unnecessary. Cut it.

---

### "The Scene Drags"

**Symptom:** Scene goes on too long. Reader's attention wanders.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are you entering late enough?
- Are you leaving early enough?
- Is there repetition (same beat hit twice)?
- Are you overwriting description or interiority?

**Fix:**
1. Cut the first paragraph. Start where the action starts.
2. Cut the last paragraph. End on the strongest beat.
3. Collapse similar beats. Two arguments? Make it one.
4. Reduce description to essential sensory details.
5. Convert introspection to action or dialogue.

---

### "The Scene Feels Rushed"

**Symptom:** Big moment happens too fast. Reader doesn't feel the weight.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Did you show the buildup?
- Did you give the moment room to breathe?
- Are you telling instead of showing?

**Fix:**
1. Add a beat before the climactic moment (the pause before the punch)
2. Slow down through sensory detail (what they see, hear, feel)
3. Show physical/emotional reaction
4. Remove the summary sentence that names the emotion

---

## DIALOGUE PROBLEMS

### "Dialogue Sounds Stilted"

**Symptom:** Characters sound like they're reading from a script.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are characters saying exactly what they mean?
- Do all characters sound the same?
- Is anyone interrupting, deflecting, or evading?

**Fix:**
1. Add subtext. What do they want but won't say?
2. Add interruptions. Real people don't wait for complete sentences.
3. Differentiate voices. Give each character a verbal quirk or pattern.
4. Cut formal phrasing. Use contractions. Fragment sentences.
5. Read aloud. If it sounds weird spoken, rewrite it.

---

### "Dialogue is On-the-Nose"

**Symptom:** Characters state their feelings and motivations explicitly.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Could you remove the dialogue and show it through action?
- Are characters arguing about the real issue or dancing around it?

**Fix:**
1. Have them talk about something else while the real issue simmers
2. Show the emotion through action beats
3. Let one character say something the other doesn't respond to
4. Use denial as a tell. ("I'm not angry." *Slams door.*)

---

### "Talking Heads"

**Symptom:** Dialogue floats in a void. No sense of place or physicality.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Where are these characters?
- What are they doing while they talk?
- Have you grounded the reader in the scene?

**Fix:**
1. Add action beats between lines
2. Establish setting in first paragraph
3. Give characters something to do with their hands
4. Use environment to create subtext (nervous character rearranges objects)

---

## CHARACTER PROBLEMS

### "Character is Boring"

**Symptom:** Reader doesn't care what happens to them.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Do they have a concrete want?
- Do they have a flaw that creates problems?
- Have you shown them making choices (good or bad)?

**Fix:**
1. Give them something to lose
2. Give them a flaw that affects the plot
3. Show them making a difficult choice early
4. Show them caring about something specific
5. Give them a voice quirk, habit, or obsession

---

### "Character Feels Like a Stereotype"

**Symptom:** Character is a collection of traits, not a person.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- What contradictions does this character have?
- What surprising thing would they do that's still in character?
- What do they want that conflicts with their role?

**Fix:**
1. Add one trait that contradicts the obvious ones
2. Give them a private moment that reveals depth
3. Give them an opinion about something unrelated to plot
4. Make them wrong about something (and let it matter)

---

### "All Characters Sound the Same"

**Symptom:** Cover the dialogue tags and you can't tell who's speaking.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Do characters have different vocabularies?
- Do they have different sentence patterns?
- Do they have different things they notice/care about?

**Fix:**
1. Education → vocabulary level
2. Background → slang, references, assumptions
3. Personality → length (terse vs. verbose), rhythm
4. Stress response → what they say (or don't) under pressure
5. Create a "voice card" for each major character

---

## PACING PROBLEMS

### "The Middle Sags"

**Symptom:** Momentum dies between inciting incident and climax.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are stakes escalating or staying flat?
- Is the protagonist proactive or just reacting?
- Does each scene cause the next one?

**Fix:**
1. Add a midpoint reversal (major shift at 50%)
2. Make failures matter (not just delays, but costs)
3. Introduce new complications (not new subplots—complications)
4. Give the antagonist more presence
5. Apply the "therefore/but" test between scenes

---

### "Pacing is Too Fast"

**Symptom:** Reader feels rushed. Emotional beats don't land.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are you skipping transitional moments?
- Are characters reacting to events?
- Is there enough interiority?

**Fix:**
1. Add reaction beats after major events
2. Show processing time (character needs to absorb what happened)
3. Use sensory detail to slow key moments
4. Add a quiet scene between action scenes

---

### "Pacing is Uneven"

**Symptom:** Some parts rush, some parts drag. No rhythm.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are scene lengths varied?
- Do high-tension scenes have slower scenes before/after?
- Are you matching narrative speed to emotional importance?

**Fix:**
1. Sequence: setup (slow) → action (fast) → aftermath (slower)
2. Use short chapters to increase perceived speed
3. Cut or compress scenes that don't earn their length
4. Expand moments that need weight

---

## PROSE PROBLEMS

### "Purple Prose"

**Symptom:** Overwrought description. Too many adjectives. Reader rolls eyes.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are you using multiple modifiers per noun?
- Does every emotion have three metaphors?
- Are you showing off instead of serving the story?

**Fix:**
1. One adjective per noun (maximum)
2. One metaphor per emotion (pick the best one)
3. Cut adverbs. Find a better verb.
4. Ask: Does this detail reveal character, advance plot, or establish mood? If no, cut.

---

### "Prose is Dull"

**Symptom:** Technically correct but lifeless. Reads like a report.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are sentences varied in length and structure?
- Are you using specific details or generic ones?
- Is there a voice, or is it neutral?

**Fix:**
1. Vary sentence length aggressively
2. Replace generic words with specific ones ("oak" not "tree")
3. Add the narrator's personality (opinion, observation, quirk)
4. Read it aloud. Where does it sound dead?

---

### "Filter Words Everywhere"

**Symptom:** "She saw," "He noticed," "She felt," distancing reader from action.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Are you placing the character between reader and experience?
- Could you state the perception directly?

**Fix:**
| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| She saw the door open | The door opened |
| He heard footsteps | Footsteps echoed |
| She felt her heart race | Her heart raced |
| He noticed it was cold | Cold bit through his jacket |

---

## STRUCTURE PROBLEMS

### "The Ending Doesn't Satisfy"

**Symptom:** Story ends, but reader feels cheated.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Did you answer the central question?
- Did the protagonist's arc complete?
- Was the resolution earned (not luck, deus ex machina)?

**Fix:**
1. Ensure the protagonist causes the resolution (not coincidence)
2. Make sure skills/knowledge used in climax were set up earlier
3. Complete the internal arc (not just external)
4. Check: Is this ending "surprising yet inevitable"?

---

### "Subplots Don't Land"

**Symptom:** Subplot resolves but feels disconnected from main story.

**Diagnosis Questions:**
- Does the subplot force choices in the main plot?
- Does it illuminate theme or character?
- Does it converge with main plot toward climax?

**Fix:**
1. Create interference (subplot problem affects main plot)
2. Create parallelism (subplot mirrors main theme)
3. Make protagonist choose between subplot and main plot goals
4. Resolve subplot through main plot resolution (or vice versa)

---

## QUICK DIAGNOSTIC

When something's wrong but you're not sure what:

| If it feels... | Check for... |
|----------------|--------------|
| Boring | Missing goal, obstacle, or stakes |
| Confusing | Too much information too fast; missing context |
| Rushed | Not enough reaction/processing time |
| Slow | Repetition, overwriting, scenes that don't earn their length |
| Artificial | Subtext missing; dialogue too on-the-nose |
| Flat | Same pacing throughout; no variation in tension |
| Random | Events not connected by causation |
| Predictable | No complications, reversals, or surprises |

---

## THE REVISION HIERARCHY

Fix problems in this order:

1. **Structure** — Does the story work?
2. **Scenes** — Does each scene earn its place?
3. **Character** — Are characters compelling and distinct?
4. **Pacing** — Does the rhythm serve the story?
5. **Dialogue** — Does it sound human?
6. **Prose** — Is it clean and vivid?
7. **Polish** — Word choice, typos, consistency

Don't polish prose until the structure is right. You'll just have to cut polished scenes later.
