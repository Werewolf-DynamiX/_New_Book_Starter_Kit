# Romance & Heat Guidelines

## Romantasy-Specific Guidelines

### Fantasy Elements in First Person Present
- She doesn't explain her world to herself—weave worldbuilding naturally.
- Magic should feel visceral and physical in her body.
- Don't info-dump history; reveal through conflict.
- Her relationship with power (magic, political, personal) IS character development.

### The Romance Arc
- Tension requires obstacles—internal and external.
- Physical awareness should build: first noticing, then fixating, then craving.
- Vulnerability is the real intimacy, not just sex.
- Banter is foreplay.
- The grovel must match the wound.

### Romance Genre Requirements
- Central relationship must drive the plot, not be subplot
- Both love interests need full characterization and arcs
- The "Happily Ever After" or "Happy For Now" must feel earned through struggle
- Sexual tension requires buildup; don't rush to resolution

### Expected Romance Beats
- Meet-cute or charged first encounter
- First conflict / the thing keeping them apart
- Growing closer despite obstacles
- Dark moment / the break
- Grand gesture / the grovel
- Resolution and commitment

### Blending the Genres
- Fantasy stakes raise romance stakes (and vice versa).
- The love interest should matter to the plot, not just her heart.
- Don't let worldbuilding interrupt emotional beats.
- Don't let romance make her stupid about real danger.

## Heat Levels Reference

**Level 1 - Sweet**: Closed door, fade to black, focus on emotional intimacy.
**Level 2 - Warm**: Some sensuality, implied sex, tasteful descriptions.
**Level 3 - Hot**: Explicit scenes, detailed but not gratuitous, emotional connection emphasized.
**Level 4 - Scorching**: Very explicit, multiple scenes, kink-friendly, still character-driven.
**Level 5 - Erotica**: Sex is the plot, frequent and detailed scenes, fantasy fulfillment focus.

Default to Level 3-4 unless specified otherwise.

### Writing Heat in First Person Present
- She's experiencing it NOW—lean into immediacy.
- Her thoughts can fragment during intensity: "His mouth. Gods, his *mouth*."
- She can lose track of things: "When did he take off my—doesn't matter."
- Physical sensations anchor the scene: temperature, texture, pressure.
- Emotions during/after matter as much as mechanics.
