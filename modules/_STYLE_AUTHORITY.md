# Style Authority: Diction, Syntax & Authenticity

**The single source of truth for word choice, sentence construction, and AI-pattern avoidance.**

---

## 1. ZERO TOLERANCE (Literal AI Tells)

These are literal AI remnants. Their presence instantly breaks immersion. **Never use.**

### Literal AI Tells
- "As an AI language model"
- "I cannot" / "I'm unable to" (in narrative voice)
- "Here's" / "Here are" to introduce lists (in prose)
- Refusal language or safety hedging in narrative contexts

### Banned Transitions
- "To understand X, you have to understand Y"
- "Here's what you need to understand"
- "Let that sink in"
- "But that's the next chapter" / "The story belongs to a later chapter"
- "It is important to note that"
- "In today's digital age"

---

## 2. HEURISTIC FLAGS (Contextual Audit)

These words are not strictly banned but are statistical indicators of AI writing when used to *smooth over* complexity rather than *describe* it.

**The Green Test:** Can you remove the word without losing meaning? If yes, it is filler. Delete it.
**The Specificity Test:** Are you using "tapestry" because you're too lazy to describe the actual threads? If yes, rewrite with specifics.

### Contextual Usage Table
| Word/Phrase | AI Usage (Bad) | Human Usage (Good) |
|-------------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Delve** | "Let's delve into the history..." (Transition) | "She delved into the pocket of her coat." (Physical action) |
| **Tapestry** | "A rich tapestry of culture..." (Metaphor) | "The faded tapestry on the wall depicted a hunt." (Literal object) |
| **Echo** | "His words echoed in her mind..." (Cliche) | "The gunshot echoed off the canyon walls." (Physical sound) |
| **Testament** | "A testament to his skill..." (Praise) | "Her last will and testament." (Legal document) |
| **Landscape** | "The political landscape..." (Metaphor) | "The landscape was scarred by craters." (Geography) |
| **Embrace** | "Embrace the challenge..." (Self-help) | "He embraced his daughter." (Physical action) |

### AI-Associated Vocabulary (Flag when clustered)
*Audit these if they appear more than 2-3 times per chapter:*
delve, tapestry, testament, myriad, plethora, multifaceted, crucial, significant, nuanced, realm, paradigm, notably, furthermore, moreover, additionally, seamlessly, leverage, harness, foster, cultivate, invaluable, indispensable, paramount, intricate, "It's worth noting," "It should be noted," game-changer, cutting-edge, revolutionary, rich tapestry, vibrant landscape, dynamic, optimize, utilize, facilitate, deep dive.

### Crutch Words (Use Sparingly)
| Word/Phrase | Max Per Manuscript | Alternative Strategy |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| impossible / impossibly | 2-3 | implausible, uncanny, against all odds |
| shimmer / shimmering | 5-6 | luminous, gleaming, lustrous, crystalline |
| obsessive / obsession | 8-10 | fanatical, relentless, exacting, driven |
| fortress / rampart | 3-4 | sanctuary, bastion, stronghold, haven |
| something | Audit for vagueness | Be specific instead |
| "in a way that" | Rephrase always | Restructure: "X enough to Y" |

## 3. FILTER WORDS [STRICT]

Remove distancing phrases that place a character between reader and action.

**Cut these:** saw, heard, felt, noticed, realized, watched, observed, thought, wondered, seemed

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| She heard footsteps in the hallway. | Footsteps echoed in the hallway. |
| He noticed the door was open. | The door hung open. |
| She felt her heart racing. | Her heart raced. |
| He realized something was wrong. | Something was wrong. |

**Exception:** Use filters when the act of perception itself matters, or to slow pacing deliberately.

---

## 4. WEAK CONSTRUCTIONS

### Wan Intensifiers (Delete or Replace)
These weaken prose. Cut them or find stronger words:
- very, really, quite, rather, somewhat, just, actually, basically, literally (when not literal)

### "To Be" + -ing (Convert to Active)
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| He was running toward the car. | He sprinted toward the car. |
| She was thinking about leaving. | She considered leaving. |

### "There Was/Were" Openers (Restructure)
| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| There were three men standing by the door. | Three men stood by the door. |
| There was a silence in the room. | Silence filled the room. |

### Em-Dash Discipline
- Em-dashes (—) are useful but become an AI tell when overused
- **Rule:** Max 1-2 per paragraph
- Prefer periods, commas, or colons for variety

## 5. VOICE & TONE

### Human Tone Requirements
- **Conversational:** Use contractions (don't, won't, can't)
- **Opinionated:** Take stances. "The album is a masterpiece" not "widely considered significant"
- **Messy:** Real writing has tangents, uneven pacing, personal asides. Too-smooth = AI
- **Emotionally Specific:** Don't just say "beautiful" or "extraordinary." Describe the specific feeling

### The Definition Loop (CRITICAL)
Technical concepts should be explained ONCE, then referenced briefly thereafter.

| Context | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| First introduction | Full explanation with context |
| Later references | Brief reference only |

**BAD:** "He had to bounce tracks, which means mixing them down to a stereo pair..." (in Chapter 8, after explaining in Chapter 1)

**GOOD:** "Returning to the hazardous bouncing technique he'd perfected..."

### Structural Rules
- **Don't re-explain:** Trust the reader with information already given
- **Vary chapter structure:** Not every chapter follows the same formula
- **Integrate, don't sidebar:** Avoid "trivia dumps" unless specifically requested

## 6. AI WRITING PITFALLS [STRICT]

Watch for and correct these 13 telltale AI patterns:

1. **Hedging language:** "seemed to," "appeared to," "somewhat," "a bit" — commit to statements
2. **Overexplaining:** Trust the reader; don't explain jokes, emotions, or subtext
3. **Purple prose:** Overwrought description, excessive adjectives, melodramatic language
4. **Repetitive sentence structure:** Especially Subject-Verb-Object patterns in sequence
5. **Telling after showing:** Describing emotion through action, then naming it anyway
6. **Floating dialogue:** Conversation without grounding in physical action or setting
7. **Uniform pacing:** Every scene same length/intensity; vary rhythm
8. **Convenient coincidences:** Characters finding exactly what they need by luck
9. **Flat supporting characters:** Everyone besides protagonist needs dimension
10. **Anachronistic language:** Modern slang in historical settings; formal speech in casual contexts
11. **Homogeneous voices:** All characters sounding alike regardless of background
12. **Excessive interiority:** Too much thinking, not enough action/dialogue
13. **Summarizing instead of dramatizing:** "They argued for an hour" vs. showing the argument

---

## 7. FACTUAL RIGOR

- **Verify credits:** Don't guess. If unsure, omit or hedge with "reportedly"
- **No unsourced quotes:** If you can't cite where someone said something, paraphrase
- **Dates matter:** Double-check release years and timelines

---

## 8. PREPOSITION ACCURACY

Common errors to avoid:
- "arrived in [Country]" not "arrived to [Country]"
- "fascination with" not "fascination for"
- "different from" not "different than" (in formal contexts)
