# Worldbuilding

**Setting is not backdrop. It's a character, an antagonist, a mood, a constraint.**

---

## 1. SETTING AS CHARACTER

Your world should feel alive—with its own personality, history, and agenda.

### What Setting Does

| Function | How It Works |
|----------|--------------|
| **Atmosphere** | Physical environment creates mood (oppressive heat, endless rain, sterile silence) |
| **Constraint** | What the world allows/forbids shapes character choices |
| **Revelation** | Environment reveals character (what they notice, how they react) |
| **Conflict** | Setting itself can be antagonist (survival stories, fish-out-of-water) |
| **Theme** | World embodies thematic concerns (decay, isolation, change) |

### The "Iceberg" Principle
Know 10x more than you show. Depth implies itself.

---

## 2. SENSORY MAPPING

Every significant location should have a sensory signature.

### The Five + Two

| Sense | Questions to Answer |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Sight** | What's the light? Colors? What catches the eye? What's hidden? |
| **Sound** | What's the ambient noise? What breaks the silence? Echoes? |
| **Smell** | What lingers? What's seasonal? What's out of place? |
| **Touch** | Temperature? Humidity? Texture underfoot? In the air? |
| **Taste** | What's in the air? What does the food taste like? The water? |
| **+Proprioception** | How does the body feel here? (cramped, exposed, off-balance) |
| **+Time** | What time of day/season/year? How does time feel? (rushed, slow, suspended) |

### Location Cards

Create a sensory profile for each major location:

```
LOCATION: The Night Market

SIGHT: Paper lanterns cast orange pools; shadows move between stalls;
       steam rises from food carts
SOUND: Haggling in three languages; sizzling oil; distant music
SMELL: Grilled meat, incense, diesel from generators
TOUCH: Humid air; sticky floor; crowd press
TASTE: Smoke on the tongue; someone nearby eating something spiced
TIME: Midnight; operates until dawn; different crowds at different hours
MOOD: Electric, slightly dangerous, anonymous
```

## 3. MAGIC SYSTEM DESIGN

For fantasy worlds. (For sci-fi, substitute "technology.")

### Brandon Sanderson's Laws

1. **First Law:** An author's ability to solve problems with magic is proportional to how well the reader understands that magic.
2. **Second Law:** Limitations are more interesting than powers.
3. **Third Law:** Expand on what you have before adding something new.

### The Three Questions

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| **What can it do?** | Defines possibilities |
| **What can't it do?** | Creates tension, prevents easy solutions |
| **What does it cost?** | Creates stakes, forces choices |

### Hard vs. Soft Magic

| Type | Characteristic | Best For |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Hard** | Explicit rules, clear limits | Problem-solving, puzzle plots |
| **Soft** | Mysterious, unexplained | Atmosphere, wonder, horror |
| **Hybrid** | Some rules known, some mysterious | Most common; balance both |

### Consistency Checks [STRICT]
- Same spell/power works the same way every time
- Costs apply consistently (no free passes when convenient)
- Limitations established before they matter to plot
- No "new power" revelations that solve problems retroactively

## 4. TECHNOLOGY INTEGRATION (Sci-Fi)

### Questions to Answer

- What level? (Near-future, far-future, post-apocalyptic regression)
- What's different? (What technologies changed society most?)
- What's the same? (What human problems persist?)
- What's the cost? (Every technology has externalities)

### Common Pitfalls

- Tech as magic (no explanation, no limits)
- Modern social values in far-future settings
- "One thing different" fallacy (tech changes ripple)
- Inventor knows everything about their invention

### The Ripple Test
If you introduce one technology, ask: What else would change?

**Example:** Personal teleportation exists.
- What happens to transportation infrastructure?
- What happens to real estate values?
- What happens to crime, borders, warfare?
- What does "distance" mean in relationships?

## 5. CULTURAL DETAILS

Cultures are systems of meaning. They're not just costumes and food.

### Layers of Culture

| Layer | Examples |
|-------|----------|
| **Surface** | Clothing, food, greetings, architecture |
| **Behavioral** | Personal space, eye contact, conflict styles |
| **Values** | What's honored? Shameful? Worth dying for? |
| **Assumptions** | What goes without saying? What's "obvious"? |
| **History** | What events shaped current attitudes? |

### Building Distinct Cultures

1. Start with a core tension or value (honor vs. survival, tradition vs. innovation)
2. Work outward: How does this value manifest in daily life?
3. Create internal contradictions (cultures aren't monoliths)
4. Define how they see outsiders (and how outsiders see them)
5. Establish taboos and aspirations

### Avoiding Monocultures
Real cultures have:
- Regional variations
- Class differences
- Generational divides
- Counter-cultures
- Internal debates

## 6. HISTORY AND LORE

### How Much History?
Enough to explain the present. Not a textbook.

### Types of Historical Information

| Type | How to Deliver |
|------|----------------|
| **Essential** | Woven into narrative naturally |
| **Atmospheric** | Implied through details, ruins, references |
| **Supplemental** | Appendices, epigraphs, in-world documents |
| **Writer's notes** | Never. (Just for your reference) |

### The "Therefore/But" Test
History should create present story. Ask:
- Because of [historical event], THEREFORE [present situation]
- The world was [way], BUT [change happened], SO NOW [current state]

## 7. INFO-DUMP ALTERNATIVES

Never pause the story to explain the world. Integrate.

### Techniques

| Method | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Action context** | Reveal through what characters do | Magic shown in use, not explained |
| **Conflict** | Rules revealed when violated | "You broke the Compact. You know what that means." |
| **Dialogue (natural)** | Characters discuss what they don't know | Not "As you know, Bob" |
| **Character reaction** | Show why something matters | Character's fear reveals danger |
| **Contrast** | Outsider POV highlights differences | Fish-out-of-water character |
| **Implication** | Let readers infer | "The last time someone tried that, we lost the Southern Provinces." |

### The "Three Sentences" Rule
If you need to explain something, limit to three sentences maximum. Then return to story.

## 8. CONSISTENCY TRACKING

Keep a world bible for reference. Update as you write.

### What to Track

- **Names:** People, places, things (with pronunciations)
- **Distances/Time:** Travel times, calendar systems
- **Rules:** Magic, tech, social (with exceptions)
- **Appearance:** Character descriptions, location details
- **Timeline:** When events happen relative to each other
- **Terminology:** Invented words and what they mean

### Red Flag Checklist

- [ ] Character travels faster than established
- [ ] Magic works differently than before
- [ ] Character knows something they shouldn't yet
- [ ] Physical description contradicts earlier
- [ ] Season/weather inconsistent with timeline
- [ ] Social rules violated without consequence

---

## 9. SHOW THE WORLD THROUGH ACTION

**Every worldbuilding detail should be earned through story.**

### The Integration Test
For each worldbuilding element, ask:
1. Does it serve plot, character, or theme?
2. Can I reveal it through action rather than explanation?
3. Does the reader need to know this NOW?

If no to all three: cut it or save it for later.

### Examples

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| Explaining the caste system | Character being treated according to caste |
| Describing the war's history | Character referencing a battle scar, a dead friend |
| Defining magic rules | Character hitting the limits, paying the cost |
| Describing architecture | Character navigating the space, noticing what matters to them |

## 10. CONTEMPORARY & MICRO-SETTINGS

Not all worldbuilding requires magic systems. Contemporary fiction needs its own worldbuilding discipline.

### Micro-Settings

Small, specific environments that become characters themselves:

| Setting Type | Worldbuilding Focus |
|-------------|---------------------|
| **Corporate office** | Hierarchy, jargon, rituals, unwritten rules |
| **High school** | Social strata, territory, generational markers |
| **Small town** | History, grudges, who knows whom, secrets |
| **Hospital/ER** | Protocols, shift culture, gallows humor |
| **Restaurant kitchen** | Chain of command, timing pressure, tribal loyalties |

### What Makes a Micro-Setting Feel Real

1. **Insider language:** Every workplace has its own vocabulary
2. **Unwritten rules:** What's technically allowed vs. what actually happens
3. **Physical geography:** Who sits where, what spaces mean what
4. **Time rhythms:** Rush periods, dead zones, rituals
5. **Status markers:** What signals power in this specific world

### The "Specific Detail" Principle

Generic details feel like research. Specific details feel like experience.

| Generic | Specific |
|---------|----------|
| "The office was busy" | "The printer queue was backed up to Tuesday" |
| "It was a small town" | "Two stoplights, one diner, and a gas station that closed at 6" |
| "The school was cliquey" | "You could map the cafeteria by SAT score" |

### Era-Specific Contemporary

For stories set in recent decades (not historical, not present):

- **Technology markers:** What phones, what music, what internet?
- **Cultural references:** What was on TV? What were people worried about?
- **What's missing:** What hadn't been invented/normalized yet?
- **Avoid anachronism:** No smartphones in 2003; no streaming in 2008

### Research Integration

For contemporary settings you don't personally know:
- Interviews over articles (texture, not facts)
- YouTube for visual/audio environment
- Reddit/forums for insider complaints and jargon
- Primary sources over summaries
